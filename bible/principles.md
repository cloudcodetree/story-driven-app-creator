# Product Bible: Core Principles

## The Golden Rules

### 1. Listen to Problems, Never Solutions
When a user describes what they want to build, dig into **why** they want it. The problem they're
solving is the truth. Their proposed solution is just one possible answer. Always redirect from
"I want feature X" to "What problem does feature X solve for you?"

### 2. Who Pays Before What to Build
Before exploring features, functionality, or tech: identify who would pay money for this.
If nobody would pay, that doesn't kill the idea — but it reshapes everything. Is it ad-supported?
Open source with services? A loss leader? Know the business model early.

### 3. If Your Target User Is Everyone, You Have No Target User
Force specificity. "People who need to organize things" is not a target user.
"Freelance designers juggling 5+ client projects" is. The narrower the target, the sharper the product.

### 4. Validate the Problem Exists Outside Your Head
Just because you have this problem doesn't mean enough other people do.
Ask: How are people solving this today? If the answer is "they aren't," that's either a huge
opportunity or a sign the problem isn't painful enough to solve.

### 5. Solve One Problem Exceptionally Before Solving Two Problems Adequately
Scope kills products. A tool that does one thing brilliantly will beat a tool that does
five things okay. Always ask: "What is the ONE thing this must nail?"

## Market Awareness

### Competition Is Good
If competitors exist, the market is validated. The question isn't "does competition exist?"
but "what's the gap?" Why are existing users unsatisfied? What's underserved?

### Switching Cost Is Real
People won't switch from an okay solution to a slightly better one. You need to be
**10x better** at one specific thing, or serve a segment that's **completely ignored**.

### Timing Matters More Than Ideas
Why is NOW the right time for this? What changed — new technology, new regulation,
new behavior, new platform? If nothing changed, ask why this hasn't been built already.

## Common Pitfalls by Category

### Marketplace Ideas
- **Chicken-and-egg problem**: You need buyers to attract sellers and sellers to attract buyers.
  Always ask: which side do you bootstrap first, and how?
- **Leakage**: Once buyers and sellers connect, what stops them from transacting off-platform?
- **Trust**: How do you handle disputes, quality, fraud?

### Social/Community Ideas
- **Cold start problem**: The product is useless with zero users. How do you create value
  for the first 10 users before network effects kick in?
- **Content moderation**: At scale, who moderates? What are the policies?
- **Engagement vs. value**: Is this genuinely useful, or just another attention trap?

### Developer Tool Ideas
- **"I'll just open source it"**: Open source is a distribution strategy, not a business model.
  How do you sustain development?
- **Integration burden**: Developers won't adopt tools that require major workflow changes.
  How does this fit into what they already use?
- **Documentation is the product**: A dev tool with bad docs is a dead tool.

### SaaS Ideas
- **Churn is the silent killer**: How sticky is this? What makes users stay month after month?
- **Feature creep**: Adding features to reduce churn usually accelerates it. Focus on core value.
- **Pricing is positioning**: Your price tells users what category you're in. Too cheap = toy.
  Too expensive = enterprise. Match your audience.

### Mobile App Ideas
- **App fatigue is real**: People install ~0 new apps per month. Why would someone install this?
- **Notification strategy**: Too many = uninstall. Too few = forgotten. What's the hook?
- **Offline capability**: Does this need to work without internet? Many mobile contexts are spotty.

## Anti-Patterns

### The Solution Looking for a Problem
"I learned this cool technology and want to build something with it."
Technology should serve the problem, not the other way around.

### The Feature Factory
"It needs to have X, Y, Z, A, B, C..." — Stop. What's the ONE feature that makes
this worth existing? Ship that. Learn. Then decide what's next.

### The Perfect Launch
"I need to build everything before I can show it to anyone."
No. Build the smallest thing that tests your riskiest assumption. Ship it. Learn.

### Building for Yourself Without Validating
"I have this problem so others must too."
Maybe. But validate. Talk to 5 people who aren't you.

### Premature Scaling
"We need to handle millions of users from day one."
No, you need to handle 10 users who love it. Then 100. Then 1,000. Scale when it hurts.

### The Pivot Trap
"If this doesn't work, we'll just pivot."
A pivot isn't a backup plan — it's an admission that your assumptions were wrong.
Better to validate assumptions cheaply upfront than to plan for failure.
